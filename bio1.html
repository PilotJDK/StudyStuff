Unit 1: Biochemistry

Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the regulation of internal conditions (e.g., temperature, pH, glucose) to maintain stability compatible with life. It relies on feedback mechanisms that detect deviations and initiate responses to restore set points.

  • Components of control systems: Stimulus (change), Receptor (senses change), Control center (compares to set point; typically brain/endocrine), Effector (executes response).
  • Set point vs. range: A target value (e.g., ~37°C body temp) with an acceptable range; oscillations around the set point are normal.
  • Negative feedback: The response opposes the initial change, reducing the deviation and stabilizing the system.
    • Blood glucose regulation: High blood glucose → pancreas secretes insulin → cells uptake glucose; low blood glucose → pancreas releases glucagon → liver releases glucose.
    • Thermoregulation: High temp → sweating/vasodilation; low temp → shivering/vasoconstriction.
    • Thyroid axis: Hypothalamus (TRH) → pituitary (TSH) → thyroid (T3/T4); high T3/T4 suppress upstream signals.
  • Positive feedback: The response amplifies the change, driving processes to completion (less common in homeostasis).
    • Childbirth: Cervical stretch → oxytocin release → stronger contractions → more stretch (loop continues until birth).
    • Blood clotting: Platelet activation recruits more platelets to rapidly seal wounds.
  • Homeostatic variables and sensors: Chemoreceptors (CO₂/O₂), osmoreceptors (osmolarity), baroreceptors (blood pressure), thermoreceptors (temperature).
  • pH balance: Buffers (e.g., bicarbonate in blood) resist pH changes by reversible binding of H⁺; enzymes require narrow pH ranges.
  • Water and osmotic balance: Aquaporins facilitate water movement; osmolarity gradients drive osmosis to equalize solute concentrations.
  • Homeostasis at cellular level: Membrane transport, ion pumps (Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase), intracellular buffers, chaperones (protein stability).
  • Dysregulation: Chronic hyperglycemia (diabetes), hypertension, acidosis/alkalosis, heat stroke/hypothermia indicate failed regulation.
Biomolecules / Macromolecules

Biomolecules are carbon-based compounds essential to life. The four major classes—carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids—have distinct monomers, structures, and functions. Organic molecules primarily contain carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O), nitrogen (N), phosphorus (P), and sulfur (S).

  • Carbohydrates: Energy storage and structural roles.
    • Monomers: Monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose).
    • Bond: Glycosidic linkage (formed via dehydration synthesis; broken by hydrolysis).
    • Types: Disaccharides (sucrose, lactose), polysaccharides (starch in plants; glycogen in animals; cellulose for plant cell walls; chitin in fungi/arthropods).
    • Starch vs. cellulose: Both glucose polymers; differing glycosidic bonds (α vs. β) change digestibility and structure.
    • Function: Rapid energy (glucose), short-/long-term storage (glycogen/starch), structure (cellulose).
  • Lipids: Hydrophobic molecules (insoluble in water) for long-term energy, membrane structure, signaling.
    • Types: Triglycerides (glycerol + 3 fatty acids), phospholipids (membranes; hydrophilic head + hydrophobic tails), steroids (cholesterol, hormones), waxes (protective coatings).
    • Saturated vs. unsaturated: Saturated (no double bonds; solid at room temp), unsaturated (≥1 double bond; kinks; liquid oils).
    • Membranes: Phospholipid bilayer forms selectively permeable barriers; cholesterol modulates fluidity.
    • Functions: Energy storage, insulation, buoyancy, signaling (steroid hormones), waterproofing (waxes).
  • Proteins: Workhorse polymers performing structural, catalytic, transport, signaling, and defense roles.
    • Monomers: Amino acids (20 standard; R-group determines properties).
    • Bond: Peptide bond (dehydration synthesis between amino and carboxyl groups).
    • Structure: Primary (sequence), secondary (α-helix/β-sheet via H-bonds), tertiary (3D folding via hydrophobic interactions, ionic bonds, H-bonds, disulfide bridges), quaternary (multiple polypeptides).
    • Functions: Enzymes, channels/transporters, receptors, antibodies, cytoskeleton, hormones (e.g., insulin).
    • Denaturation: Loss of shape and function due to heat, pH, solvents; may be reversible or irreversible.
  • Nucleic acids: Information storage and transfer.
    • Monomers: Nucleotides (sugar + phosphate + nitrogenous base).
    • DNA: Deoxyribose sugar; bases A, T, C, G; double-stranded antiparallel helix; complementary base pairing (A–T, C–G).
    • RNA: Ribose sugar; bases A, U, C, G; typically single-stranded; types include mRNA, tRNA, rRNA; roles in protein synthesis and regulation.
    • Phosphodiester bonds: Link sugar-phosphate backbone; 5′→3′ directionality critical for replication/transcription.
  • Functional groups: Hydroxyl (–OH), carboxyl (–COOH), amino (–NH₂), phosphate (–PO₄²⁻), carbonyl (C=O), sulfhydryl (–SH) dictate reactivity and interactions.
  • Polymerization: Dehydration synthesis builds polymers; hydrolysis breaks them into monomers.
  • Atoms and bonds: Covalent bonds (strong), hydrogen bonds (moderate; vital in H₂O, DNA), ionic interactions (charged groups), van der Waals forces (weak, cumulative).
  • Water properties: Polarity, hydrogen bonding, cohesion/adhesion, high specific heat, high heat of vaporization, universal solvent; critical for biochemical reactions and temperature buffering.
Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts (mostly proteins; some RNA ribozymes) that increase reaction rates by lowering activation energy. They do not alter overall ΔG or the reaction's equilibrium—only the speed at which equilibrium is reached.

  • Active site: Region where substrates bind; specificity arises from shape and chemical environment (induced fit fine-tunes interactions).
  • Substrates → products: Enzymes stabilize transition states and orient substrates to facilitate bond breaking/forming.
  • Optimum conditions: Each enzyme has an optimal temperature and pH for maximal activity; deviations reduce rate or cause denaturation.
    • Temperature: Increasing temp generally increases rate (to a point) by kinetic energy; excessive heat disrupts bonds → denaturation.
    • pH: Alters ionization of active-site residues and substrate; extreme pH disrupts ionic/H-bonds → denaturation.
  • Denaturation: Loss of 3D structure (and function) due to heat, extreme pH, solvents, heavy metals; may be irreversible.
  • Kinetics: Reaction rate depends on substrate concentration, enzyme concentration, and environmental conditions.
    • Saturation: At high substrate, active sites are fully occupied; rate plateaus at Vmax.
    • Michaelis–Menten (conceptual): Km reflects substrate concentration at half Vmax; lower Km indicates higher affinity.
  • Regulation: Cells modulate enzyme activity by inhibitors, activators, cofactors, coenzymes, and post-translational modifications.
    • Inhibitors: Competitive (bind active site; overcome by more substrate) vs noncompetitive (bind allosteric site; reduce Vmax).
    • Allosteric regulation: Effector binding changes conformation and activity (feedback inhibition common in pathways).
    • Cofactors/coenzymes: Non-protein helpers (metal ions, vitamins-derived molecules like NAD⁺/FAD) essential for catalysis.
  • Reaction terms: Reactant/substrate (starting chemical), product (result), catalyst (accelerates without being consumed), activation energy (energy barrier to start reaction).
  • Assays and experimental tests:
    • Rate measurement: Track substrate disappearance or product appearance over time (colorimetric, fluorometric).
    • Effect of variables: Systematically vary temperature, pH, substrate/enzyme concentration, or inhibitors; plot rate vs variable.
    • Controls: Include negative controls (no enzyme) and positive controls (known active enzyme) to validate results.
    • Standard curves: Convert absorbance/fluorescence to concentration for quantitative analysis.
  • Real-world examples: Amylase (starch → maltose), lactase (lactose → glucose/galactose), DNA polymerase (adds nucleotides), catalase (H₂O₂ → H₂O + O₂).

Unit 2: Cellular Structure and Function

Cell Theory

All living things are made of cells, cells are the basic unit of life, and all cells come from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

Prokaryotes lack membrane-bound organelles; eukaryotes have them. Classification based on traits like nucleus presence, complexity, and size.

Organelles

Nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, Golgi, lysosomes, chloroplasts. Dysfunction leads to disease. Each has a specific role in cell function.

Cell Differentiation & Specialization

Cells specialize to perform unique functions (e.g., muscle vs nerve cells). Stem cells can differentiate into many types.

Lab Safety

Microscope handling, proper use of lab tools, and safety protocols to prevent accidents.

Unit 3: DNA and Mitosis

DNA

Double helix structure. Replication is semi-conservative. RNA is a related nucleic acid used in protein synthesis.

Cell Cycle

Includes interphase, mitosis, cytokinesis. Errors can lead to cancer. Mitosis ensures identical diploid cells.

Biotechnology

DNA fingerprinting, gel electrophoresis, genetic engineering, GMOs, and transgenic organisms.

Unit 4: Cell Transport and Energy

Plasma Membrane

Semipermeable barrier with protein channels. Maintains homeostasis by controlling entry/exit of molecules.

Transport

Passive transport (diffusion, osmosis) vs active transport (requires ATP). Maintains balance in cells.

Photosynthesis & Respiration

Photosynthesis produces glucose and oxygen; respiration (aerobic and anaerobic) produces ATP. Includes glycolysis, Krebs cycle, electron transport chain.

Bioenergetics

Study of energy flow in living systems. Links photosynthesis and respiration as complementary processes.

Unit 5: Ecology

Cycles

Nitrogen, phosphorus, water, oxygen, and carbon cycles recycle matter through ecosystems.

Ecosystems

Energy flows through trophic levels (producers, consumers, decomposers). Classification of ecosystem layers.

Human Impact

Pollution, deforestation, climate change, and biodiversity loss affect ecosystems.

Unit 6: Protein Synthesis and Genetic Variability

DNA Biotechnology

Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments. Used in forensics and genetic research.

Protein Synthesis

Transcription (DNA → mRNA) and translation (mRNA → protein at ribosomes).

Meiosis

Produces gametes (haploid). Crossing over increases genetic diversity. Fertilization forms zygotes.

DNA Mutations

Point and frameshift mutations alter genetic code. Chromosomal changes affect variation. Replication is semi-conservative.

Unit 7: Heredity

Genetics Basics

Monohybrid crosses, incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, sex-linked traits.

Pedigrees & Polygenic Traits

Pedigrees trace inheritance. Polygenic traits involve multiple genes (e.g., height, skin color).

Cell Cycle & Reproduction

Meiosis terms: haploid, diploid, zygote, gamete, nondisjunction. Genotype vs phenotype, homozygous vs heterozygous.

Karyotypes & Syndromes

Chromosome analysis reveals conditions like Down syndrome, Klinefelter, dwarfism, Huntington's, PKU.

Unit 8: Evolution and Classification

Dichotomous Keys

Tools for identifying organisms based on traits.

Natural Selection

Adaptation, fitness, resistance. Darwin's theory explains survival of the fittest.

Evolution Concepts

Endosymbiosis, LUCA, common ancestor, cladograms, phylogenetic trees. Contributions of Darwin and Lamarck.

Isolation Mechanisms

Geographic, reproductive, and behavioral isolation drive speciation.

Evidence of Evolution

Homologous, analogous, and vestigial structures. Fossil record supports evolutionary theory.
