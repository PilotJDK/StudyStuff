Unit 1: Biochemistry

Homeostasis

Homeostasis is the biological process by which organisms maintain stable internal conditions despite external environmental changes. This regulation keeps critical variables such as body temperature, blood pH, water balance, and glucose concentration within narrow limits that allow enzymes and cells to function properly. Homeostasis operates through feedback loops that involve receptors detecting change, control centers processing that information, and effectors carrying out responses. Negative feedback loops are the most common and counteract change, such as insulin lowering blood sugar or sweating cooling the body. Positive feedback loops, which are less common, amplify change to drive a process to completion, such as oxytocin release during childbirth or the cascade of reactions involved in blood clotting. Failure of homeostatic systems results in disorders such as diabetes, hypertension, hypothermia, or acidosis.

Biomolecules / Macromolecules

Living organisms are built from carbon-based molecules known as biomolecules, which include carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Carbohydrates function primarily as short- and long-term energy sources and structural components. Lipids provide long-term energy storage, insulation, and form the basis of cell membranes through phospholipid bilayers. Proteins, built from amino acids, perform most cellular work including transport, signaling, structure, defense, and chemical reactions. Nucleic acids such as DNA and RNA store and transmit genetic information. These macromolecules are formed by dehydration synthesis and broken down by hydrolysis. The unique chemical properties of water, including polarity and hydrogen bonding, make it essential for the structure and function of these molecules.

Enzymes

Enzymes are biological catalysts that accelerate chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required for a reaction to occur. Most enzymes are proteins, although some RNA molecules can also act as catalysts. Each enzyme has a specific active site that binds substrates and facilitates their conversion into products. Enzyme activity is highly dependent on temperature and pH, with each enzyme having optimal conditions at which it works best. High temperatures or extreme pH can denature enzymes by disrupting their structure. Enzymes are essential for metabolism and can be regulated by inhibitors, activators, and cofactors to control the rates of biochemical pathways.

Unit 2: Cellular Structure and Function

Cell Theory

Cell theory states that all living organisms are composed of one or more cells, that the cell is the basic unit of structure and function, and that all cells arise from pre-existing cells. This theory forms the foundation of modern biology and explains growth, development, and repair in living systems.

Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells

Cells are classified based on structural complexity. Prokaryotic cells, such as bacteria, lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles, while eukaryotic cells contain a nucleus and specialized organelles. These structural differences influence cell size, metabolism, and reproductive strategies, with eukaryotic cells typically being larger and more compartmentalized.

Organelles

Eukaryotic cells contain membrane-bound organelles that carry out specialized functions. The nucleus controls cellular activities and stores DNA, mitochondria generate ATP through cellular respiration, ribosomes synthesize proteins, the endoplasmic reticulum transports and modifies proteins and lipids, and the Golgi apparatus packages and ships materials. Lysosomes digest wastes and damaged organelles, while chloroplasts perform photosynthesis in plant cells. Malfunction of these structures can disrupt cell function and contribute to disease.

Cell Differentiation & Specialization

Cell differentiation is the process by which unspecialized cells become specialized to perform specific tasks. Through changes in gene expression, cells develop unique structures that allow them to carry out functions such as muscle contraction, nerve signaling, or nutrient absorption. Stem cells maintain the ability to divide and differentiate and play a key role in growth, development, and tissue repair.

Lab Safety

Laboratory safety is critical for preventing injuries and maintaining a controlled environment for scientific work. Proper microscope handling, correct use of chemicals and equipment, use of safety goggles and gloves, and adherence to established protocols reduce the risk of accidents and contamination.

Unit 3: DNA and Mitosis

DNA

DNA is a double-stranded molecule that stores the genetic instructions for the development, functioning, and reproduction of living organisms. Its double helix structure allows it to replicate accurately through a semi-conservative process, ensuring genetic continuity. RNA differs from DNA in structure and function and plays a central role in protein production.

Cell Cycle

The cell cycle is a tightly regulated series of events that leads to cell growth and division. Interphase prepares the cell by replicating DNA and producing organelles, while mitosis and cytokinesis result in two genetically identical daughter cells. When regulation fails, cells may divide uncontrollably, leading to cancer.

Biotechnology

Biotechnology involves manipulating living organisms or their systems to develop useful products and technologies. Methods such as DNA fingerprinting, gel electrophoresis, recombinant DNA technology, genetic engineering, and the production of GMOs and transgenic organisms are used in medicine, agriculture, and criminal investigations.

Unit 4: Cell Transport and Energy

Plasma Membrane

The plasma membrane is a selectively permeable structure composed of a phospholipid bilayer embedded with proteins, cholesterol, and carbohydrates. It controls the movement of substances into and out of the cell, allowing the cell to maintain internal balance and communicate with its environment.

Transport

Cells transport materials across membranes through passive and active processes. Passive transport, including diffusion, facilitated diffusion, and osmosis, does not require energy and moves substances along concentration gradients. Active transport requires ATP and moves substances against their concentration gradients to maintain cellular equilibrium.

Photosynthesis & Respiration

Photosynthesis captures light energy to produce glucose and oxygen, while cellular respiration releases stored energy by breaking down glucose to produce ATP. This process occurs through glycolysis, the Krebs cycle, and the electron transport chain and can function aerobically or anaerobically depending on oxygen availability.

Bioenergetics

Bioenergetics is the study of how energy flows through living systems, linking the processes of photosynthesis and cellular respiration to maintain the energy needs of organisms.

Unit 5: Ecology

Cycles

Biogeochemical cycles, including the water, carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, and phosphorus cycles, describe how matter is recycled through the atmosphere, biosphere, hydrosphere, and geosphere. These cycles are essential for maintaining ecosystem balance and nutrient availability.

Ecosystems

Ecosystems consist of interactions between living organisms-– such as producers, consumers, and decomposers––and their physical environment. Energy moves through trophic levels and food webs, while matter is recycled within the system.

Human Impact

Human activities such as pollution, deforestation, urban development, and climate change have significantly altered natural ecosystems, leading to habitat loss, species extinction, and changes in global climate patterns.

Unit 6: Protein Synthesis and Genetic Variability

DNA Biotechnology

DNA biotechnology uses laboratory techniques to analyze and manipulate genetic material. Gel electrophoresis separates DNA fragments by size and charge and is widely used in forensic science, medical research, and genetic engineering.

Protein Synthesis

Protein synthesis involves transcription, where DNA is used to create mRNA, and translation, where ribosomes read the mRNA to assemble amino acids into a functional protein.

Meiosis

Meiosis is a specialized type of cell division that produces haploid gametes and increases genetic diversity through crossing over and independent assortment. Fertilization restores the diploid chromosome number in the zygote.

DNA Mutations

Mutations include point mutations and frameshift mutations that alter DNA sequences and can affect protein function. Structural and numerical chromosome changes increase genetic variation and can lead to disease.

Unit 7: Heredity

Genetics Basics

Heredity is the transmission of genetic traits from parents to offspring. Inheritance patterns include simple dominant-recessive traits, incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, and sex-linked traits.

Pedigrees & Polygenic Traits

Pedigrees are diagrams used to track the inheritance of traits across multiple generations. Polygenic traits, such as height and skin color, are influenced by multiple genes and show a wide range of variation.

Cell Cycle & Reproduction

Sexual reproduction relies on meiosis to produce haploid gametes. Key terms include haploid, diploid, zygote, gamete, nondisjunction, genotype, phenotype, homozygous, and heterozygous. Errors in chromosome separation can lead to genetic disorders.

Karyotypes & Syndromes

Karyotypes are visual displays of chromosomes used to detect genetic conditions such as Down syndrome, Klinefelter syndrome, Huntington’s disease, dwarfism, and phenylketonuria (PKU).

Unit 8: Evolution and Classification

Dichotomous Keys

Dichotomous keys are step-by-step tools used to identify organisms by comparing observable physical characteristics.

Natural Selection

Natural selection is the process by which individuals with advantageous traits survive and reproduce at higher rates, increasing the frequency of those traits in a population over time. Concepts such as fitness, resistance, and adaptation explain how species evolve.

Evolution Concepts

Evolution explains the diversity of life through descent with modification from a common ancestor. Concepts include endosymbiotic theory, the last universal common ancestor (LUCA), cladograms, and phylogenetic trees. Scientists such as Charles Darwin and Jean-Baptiste Lamarck contributed to evolutionary thought, although Darwin’s model is supported by modern evidence.

Isolation Mechanisms

Speciation occurs when populations become reproductively isolated through geographic barriers, behavioral differences, or biological incompatibility, preventing gene flow between groups.

Evidence of Evolution

Fossil records and comparative anatomy provide strong evidence for evolution. Homologous structures indicate shared ancestry, analogous structures show convergent evolution, and vestigial structures are remnants of ancestral traits.
